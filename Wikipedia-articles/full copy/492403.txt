[ 1950 09 07 ] { Brooklyn, New York } { American } { Presidential Speechwriter } { Author, columnist, speechwriter } Peggy Noonan (born Margaret Ellen Noonan on September 7, 1950, in Brooklyn, New York ) is an American author of seven books on politics, religion, and culture and a weekly columnist for The Wall Street Journal. She was a primary speech writer and Special Assistant to President Ronald Reagan and in her political writings is considered a Republican. She is a graduate of Rutherford High School in Rutherford, New Jersey, and Fairleigh Dickinson University. { About Rutherford High School, Rutherford High School. Accessed July 7, 2007. &quot; Career diplomat and ambassador Thomas H. Pickering and presidential speechwriter Peggy Noonan are among those honored as part of this tradition. &quot; } Five of Noonan&apos;s books have been New York Times bestsellers. Noonan is a trustee of the Manhattan Institute. She has been awarded honorary doctorates from Miami University; St. John Fisher College; her alma mater, Fairleigh Dickinson University; Adelphi College; and Saint Francis College. She was nominated for an Emmy Award for her work on America: A Tribute to Heroes. She has also been widely recognized for her regular appearances on ABC&apos;s This Week and NBC&apos;s Meet The Press. In her political writings, Noonan frequently cites the political figures she admires, including Ronald Reagan, Abraham Lincoln, and Edmund Burke. [ Personal ] Noonan married Richard W. Rahn, who was then chief economist at the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, in 1985. They lived in Great Falls, Virginia. Their son was born in 1987. { http://www.time.com/time/magazine/article/0,9171,967310,00.html Hugh Sidey, &quot; The Presidency: Of Poets and Word Processors, } { Time (magazine), May 2, 1988. } Noonan and her husband were divorced after five years of marriage. In 1989 she returned with her son to her native New York. In 2004, according to an interview with Crisis Magazine, she lived in a brownstone in Brooklyn Heights with her son, who attended the nearby Saint Ann&apos;s School. { Anne Morse, &quot; Meeting Peggy Noonan, &quot; &apos;&apos;Crisis Magazine&apos;&apos;, September, 2004. } [ September 2011 ] Noonan currently lives in New York City. { http://tobaccodocuments.org/nysa_ti_s3/TI46320030.html?zoom=750 &amp; ocr_position=above_foramatted &amp; start_page=41 Margaret Rahn in Busch/Quayle (sic) Alumni Directory. } In 2010 she bought an apartment on the Upper East Side of Manhattan. { Harris, Elizabeth A., &quot; New Taxes For Peggy Noonan &quot; item, &quot; Big Deal &quot; column, page 2 of the &quot; Real Estate &quot; section, The New York Times, April 18, 2010 } Noonan is Roman Catholic. She also wrote Reagan&apos;s address to the nation after the Challenger explosion, drawing upon the poet John Magee &apos;s famous words about aviators who &quot; slipped the surly bonds of earth... and touched the face of God. &quot; The latter is ranked as one of the ten best American political speeches of the 20th century according to a list compiled by professors at the University of Wisconsin-Madison and Texas A &amp; M University and based on the opinions of &quot; 137 leading scholars of American public address. &quot; The &quot; Pointe du Hoc &quot; speech ranks as the 60th best speech of the century. Kennedy at a fundraising event held at the McLean, Virginia, home of Senator Edward M. Kennedy in the spring of 1984. Later, while working for then Vice President George H. W. Bush, Noonan coined the phrase &quot; a kinder, gentler nation &quot; and also popularized &quot; a thousand points of light, &quot; two memorable catchphrase s used by Bush. Noonan also wrote the speech in which Bush pledged: &quot; Read my lips: no new taxes &quot; during his 1988 presidential nomination acceptance speech in New Orleans (Bush&apos;s subsequent reversal of this pledge is often cited as a reason for his defeat in his 1992 re-election campaign ). [ Career ] Before the Reagan years, Noonan worked as the daily CBS Radio commentary writer for anchorman Dan Rather at CBS News, whom she once called &quot; the best boss I ever had. &quot; From 1975 through 1977 she worked the overnight shift as a newswriter at WEEI Radio in Boston, where she was later Editorial and Public Affairs Director. In 1978 and 1979 she was an adjunct professor of journalism at New York University. [ http://online.wsj.com/public/page/peggy-noonan.html Peggy Noonan - Wsj.com Online.wsj.com 2011-09-26 ] Noonan also worked as a consultant on the American television drama The West Wing. In mid August 2004, Noonan took a brief unpaid leave from the Wall Street Journal to campaign for George W. Bush &apos;s reelection. Noonan became increasingly critical of the Bush administration after Bush&apos;s inaugural address in January 2005.
