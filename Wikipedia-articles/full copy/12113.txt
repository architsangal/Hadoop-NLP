[ Service sector ] [ Maritime industry ] [ Greek shipping List of ports in Greece ] { Greece controls 16.2% of the world&apos;s total } { merchant fleet, making it the largest in the world. Greece is ranked in the top 5 for all kinds of ships, including first for tankers and bulk carriers. } Shipping has traditionally been a key sector in the Greek economy since ancient times. [ http://www.greece.org/poseidon/work/articles/polemis_one.html The History of Greek Shipping Polemis, Spyros M. greece.org 9 April 2007 ] In 1813, the Greek merchant navy was made up of 615 ships. [ Ιστορία των Ελλήνων – Ο Ελληνισμός υπό Ξένη Κυριαρχία 1453–1821 History of the Greeks – Hellenism under Foreign Rule 1453–1821 Domi Publishings Athens 960-8177-93-6 652–653 Volume 8 ] Its total tonnage was 153,580 tons and was manned with 37,526 crewmembers and 5,878 cannons. During the 1960s, the size of the Greek fleet nearly doubled, primarily through the investment undertaken by the shipping magnates Onassis and Niarchos. [ http://slate.msn.com/id/2124542 So Many Greek Shipping Magnates  ... Engber Daniel Slate Washington Post/slate.msn.com 17 August 2005 9 April 2007 ] The basis of the modern Greek maritime industry was formed after World War  II when Greek shipping businessmen were able to amass surplus ships sold to them by the United States Government through the Ship Sales Act of the 1940s. Currently Greece has the largest merchant navy in the world as a percentage of the world&apos;s total dwt, at 16.2% according to a United Nations Conference on Trade and Development 2011 report, [ http://www.unctad.org/en/docs/rmt2011_en.pdf Review of Maritime Transport 2011 United Nations 2011 17 February 2012 ] an increase from 15.98% in 2010. [ http://www.unctad.org/en/docs/rmt2010_en.pdf Review of Maritime Transport 2010 United Nations 2010 10 August 2011 ] Although a drop from the capacity of 18.2% of the world&apos;s total that the country&apos;s merchant fleet controlled in 2006, [ http://www.unctad.org/en/docs/rmt2006_en.pdf Review of Maritime Transport 2006 United Nations 2006 10 August 2011 ] the Greek Merchant Navy is still the largest, but followed closely by that of Japan, at 15.8%. The Greek merchant navy&apos;s total dwt is 202 million. Japan is second with 197 million, but Germany, which is ranked third, stands at 114 million. This comes to show that, although the Japanese merchant navy appears to be challenging the supremacy of the Greek one in terms of dwt, the Greek merchant navy&apos;s total dwt is not comparable to any other nation other than Japan. Additionally, Greece represents 41.49% of all of the European Union &apos;s dwt. However, today&apos;s fleet roster is smaller than an all-time high of 5,000 ships in the late 1970s. Greece is also ranked in the top 5 country merchant fleets by number of ships. In the same 2011 United Nations report, the Greek merchant navy came fourth with 3,213 ships, after Japan, China and Germany. There is a significant gap between Greece and Norway, which came fifth with 1,984 ships. A European Community Shipowners&apos; Association report for 2010-2011 reveals that the Greek flag is the fifth-most-used internationally for shipping, while it ranks first in the EU. In terms of ship categories, Greece controls 22.5% of the world&apos;s tankers and 16.8% of the world&apos;s bulk carriers (in dwt). An additional equivalent of 20.05% of the world&apos;s tanker dwt is on order, with another 12.1% of bulk carriers also on order. Today, shipping is one of the country&apos;s most important industries. It accounts for 6% of GDP, [ http://www.ecsa.be/ar/Rapport%202010-2011.pdf European Community Shipowners&apos; Association ecsa.be ECSA Annual report 2010-2011 19 October 2011 ] employs about 160,000 people (4% of the workforce), [ http://www.nbg.gr/en/pr_release_resb.asp?P_ID%3D463 National Bank of Greece nbg.gr Greek shipping is modernized to remain a global leader and expand its contribution to the Greek economy 11 May 2006 8 April 2007 http://web.archive.org/web/20070831114031/http%3A//www.nbg.gr/en/pr_release_resb.asp?P_ID%3D463 31 August 2007 ] and represents 1/3 of the country&apos;s trade deficit. Earnings from shipping amounted to €15.4 billion in 2010, while between 2000 and 2010 Greek shipping contributed a total of €140 billion (half of the country&apos;s public debt in 2009 and 3.5 times the receipts from the European Union in the period 2000-2013). The same ECSA report showed that there are approximately 750 Greek shipping companies in operation. [ http://appsso.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/nui/submitViewTableAction.do?dvsc%3D9 Maritime transport - Goods (gross weight) - Annual data - All ports - by direction Eurostat 19 October 2011 ] The Port of Thessaloniki comes second with 15.8 million tons, followed by the Port of Piraeus, with 13.2 million tons, and the port of Eleusis, with 12.37 million tons. The total number of goods transported through Greece in 2010 amounted to 124.38 million tons, a considerable drop from the 164.3 million tons transported through the country in 2007. In 2010 Piraeus handled 513,319 TEU s, [ http://www.olp.gr/el/stats/viewdownload/7-/372------2007-2008-2009-2010 Ενιαίος Πίνακας Στατιστικών Στοιχείων Ετών 2007, 2008, 2009, 2010 Piraeus Port Authority 19 October 2011 ] followed by Thessaloniki, which handled 273,282 TEUs. [ http://www.thpa.gr/files/statistics/statistics2010en.pdf Statistical Data 2010 Thessaloniki Port Authority 19 October 2011 ] In the same year, 83.9 million people passed through Greece&apos;s ports, [ http://appsso.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/nui/show.do# Maritime transport - Passengers - Annual data - All ports - by direction Eurostat 19 October 2011 ] 12.7 million through the port of Paloukia in Salamis, another 12.7 through the port of Perama, 9.5 million through Piraeus and 2.7 million through Igoumenitsa. [ Energy ] { Hellenic Petroleum. } Energy production in Greece is dominated by the Public Power Corporation (known mostly by its acronym ΔΕΗ, or in English DEI). In 2009 DEI supplied for 85.6% of all energy demand in Greece, [ http://www.dei.gr/Images/ENG%20REPORT%202010%20FINAL.pdf Public Power Corporation S.A. Financial Report (January 1, 2010 - December 31, 2010) 2010 Public Power Corporation of Greece 24 October 2011 ] while the number fell to 77.3% in 2010. Almost half (48%) of DEI&apos;s power output is generated using lignite, a drop from the 51.6% in 2009. Another 12% comes from Hydroelectric power plants [ http://www.investingreece.gov.gr/default.asp?pid%3D36%26amp%3BsectorID%3D38%26amp%3Bla%3D1 Energy Invest in Greece Agency 26 October 2011 ] and another 20% from natural gas. Between 2009 and 2010, independent companies&apos; energy production increased by 56%, from 2,709 Gigawatt hour in 2009 to 4,232 GWh in 2010. In 2008 renewable energy accounted for 8% of the country&apos;s total energy consumption, [ http://epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/tgm/table.do?tab%3Dtable%26amp%3Binit%3D1%26amp%3Blanguage%3Den%26amp%3Bpcode%3Dtsdcc110%26amp%3Bplugin%3D0 Share of renewable energy in gross final energy consumption % 2008 Eurostat 24 October 2011 ] a rise from the 7.2% it accounted for in 2006, but still below the EU average of 10% in 2008. 10% of the country&apos;s renewable energy comes from solar power, [ http://epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/cache/ITY_OFFPUB/KS-78-09-865/EN/KS-78-09-865-EN.PDF Sustainable development in the European Union 2009 Eurostat 24 October 2011 ] while most comes from biomass and waste recycling. In line with the European Commission &apos;s Directive on Renewable Energy, Greece aims to get 18% of its energy from renewable sources by 2020. [ http://ec.europa.eu/energy/renewables/targets_en.htm Renewable energy &gt; &gt; Targets by 2020 Eurostat 24 October 2011 ] Greece currently does not have any nuclear power plant s in operation, however in 2009 the Academy of Athens suggested that research in the possibility of Greek nuclear power plants begin. [ http://www.academyofathens.gr/Documents/christoforou_porismata.pdf Πορίσματα της Ομάδας Εργασίας της Επιτροπής Ενέργειας της Ακαδημίας Αθηνών επί του θέματος &quot; Πυρηνική Ενέργεια και Ενεργειακές Ανάγκες της Ελλάδος &quot; Academy of Athens 24 October 2011 ] Greece has 10 million barrel s of proved oil reserves as of 1 January 2011. Hellenic Petroleum is the country&apos;s largest oil company, followed by Motor Oil Hellas. Greece&apos;s oil production stands at 7,946 barrels per day (bbl/d), ranked 90th, while it exports 181,600 bbl/d (57th) and imports 496,600 bbl/d (25th). In 2011 the Greek government approved the start of oil exploration and drilling in three locations within Greece, [ http://www.investingreece.gov.gr/default.asp?pid%3D25%26amp%3Bla%3D1%26amp%3Bn%3D1024 Green Light for Hydrocarbon Exploration Invest in Greece Agency 26 October 2011 ] with an estimated output of 250 to 300 million barrels over the next 15 to 20 years. The estimated output in Euro s of the three deposits is €25 billion over a 15-year period, of which €13–€14 billion will enter state coffers. Greece&apos;s dispute with Turkey over the Aegean poses substantial obstacles to oil exploration in the Aegean Sea. In addition to the above, Greece is also to start oil and gas exploration in other locations in the Ionian Sea as well as the Libyan Sea, within the Greek exclusive economic zone, south of Crete. A number of oil and gas pipeline s are currently under construction or under planning in the country. Such projects include the Interconnector Turkey-Greece-Italy (ITGI) and South Stream gas pipelines. [ Taxation and tax evasion ] [ Taxation in Greece ] Revenues of Greece between 1999 and 2010 as a percentage of GDP, compared to the EU average. The Greek tax system is a tiered one, as Greece employs the system of progressive taxation. Greek law recognizes six categories of taxable income: [ http://epp.eurostat.ec.europa.eu/cache/ITY_OFFPUB/KS-DU-11-001/EN/KS-DU-11-001-EN.PDF Taxation trends in the European Union 2011 Edition 2011 Eurostat 30 October 2011 ] immovable property, movable property (investment), income from agriculture, business, employment, and income from professional activities. Greece&apos;s personal income tax rate up until recently ranged from 0% for annual incomes below €12,000 and 45% for annual incomes over €100,000. Under the new 2010 tax reform, tax exemption s have been abolished. Also under the new austerity measures and among other changes, the personal income tax-free ceiling has been reduced to €5,000 per annum [ http://www.ekathimerini.com/4dcgi/_w_articles_wsite2_1_07/10/2011_409919 Tax bill to hurt lower incomes 2011 ekathimerini 5 March 2012 ] while further future changes, for example abolition of this ceiling, are already being planned. [ http://www.ekathimerini.com/4dcgi/_w_articles_wsite2_1_24/02/2012_429833 Tax burden to get bigger in June 2012 ekathimerini 5 March 2012 ] Greece&apos;s corporate tax has dropped from 40% in 2000 to 20% in 2010. For 2011 only, corporate tax will be at 24%. Value added tax (VAT) has gone up in 2010 compared to 2009: 23% as opposed to 19%. The lowest VAT possible is 6.5% (previously 4.5%) for newspapers, periodicals and cultural event tickets, while a tax rate of 13% (from 9%) applies to certain service sector professions. Additionally, both employers and employees have to pay social contribution taxes, which apply at a rate of 16% for white collar jobs and 19.5% for blue collar jobs, and are used for social insurance. Greece suffers from very high levels of tax evasion. In the last quarter of 2005, tax evasion reached 49%, [ http://www.ethnos.gr/article.asp?catid%3D22770%26amp%3Bsubid%3D2%26amp%3Bpubid%3D29345 Πτώση της φοροδιαφυγής στο 41,6% από 49% το τελευταίο εξάμηνο 2006 Ethnos 12 October 2011 ] while in January 2006 it fell to 41.6%. The Tax Justice Network has said that there are over €20 billion in Swiss bank accounts held by Greeks. [ http://www.skai.gr/news/finance/article/182717/20-dis-euro-ehoun-krupsei-oi-ellines-stin-elvetia/ 20 δισ. ευρώ έχουν κρύψει οι Έλληνες στην Ελβετία 2011 Skai TV 12 October 2011 ] The current Finance Minister of Greece, Evangelos Venizelos, was quoted as saying “Around 15,000 individuals and companies owe the taxman 37 billion euros”. [ Boyes Roger Rich greeks pack up their troubles along with their euros 20 November 2011 The Times ] Additionally, the TJN puts the number of Greek-owned off-shore companies to over 10,000. [ http://www.tanea.gr/ellada/article/?aid%3D4515967 Υπερδύναμη στις οφ σορ η Ελλάδα 2011 Ta Nea 12 October 2011 ] Following similar actions by the United Kingdom and Germany, the Greek government is in talks with Switzerland in order to tax bank accounts in Switzerland owned by Greek citizens. [ http://www.skai.gr/news/finance/article/184756/mehri-to-telos-tou-2011-i-sumfonia-gia-ti-forologisi-ton-katatheseon-stin-elvetia-/ Μέχρι το τέλος του 2011 η συμφωνία για τη φορολόγηση των καταθέσεων στην Ελβετία Deal to tax Swiss bank accounts to be reached by end of 2011 Skai TV 30 October 2011 ] The Ministry of Finance has revealed that Greek Swiss bank account holders will either have to pay a tax or reveal information such as the identity of the bank account holder to the Greek internal revenue services. The Greek and Swiss governments are to reach a deal on the matter by the end of 2011. The Ministry of Finance expects tax revenues for 2012 to be €52.7 billion (€23.6 billion in direct taxes and €29.1 billion in indirect taxes), [ http://www.minfin.gr/content-api/f/binaryChannel/minfin/datastore/03/59/99/03599935df46e7658a790bf311646f9424ec43a2/application/pdf/PROSXEDIO_2012.pdf Προϋπολογισμός 2012 2011 Ministry of Finance 12 October 2011 ] an increase of 5.8% from 2011. In 2012 the government is expected to have considerably higher tax revenues than in 2011 on a number of sectors, primarily housing (an increase of 217.5% from 2011). After having signed the Maastricht Treaty, Greece applied to join the Eurozone. The two main criteria to join the Euro currency were, that the EU country upon application time, was not allowed to exceed a public deficit of -3.0% of GDP and the debt burden should show a declining trend if it was above 60% of GDP. Greece managed to comply with the strict criteria, after having submitted its 1999 annual public account. On January 1, 2001, Greece officially joined the Eurozone, with the adoption of the Euro at the fixed exchange rate ₯340.75 to €1. In 2001 the Euro however only existed electronically, so the physical exchange from Drachma to Euro only happened on January 1, 2002. This was followed by a ten-year period for eligible exchange of drachma to Euro, which ended on 1 March 2012.
