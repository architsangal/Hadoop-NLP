Lettera 43. 13 November 2011. } { Mario Monti signature.png } Mario Monti ( [ ˈmaːrjo ˈmonti ]; born 19 March 1943) is an Italian economist and academic who has been the 54th Prime Minister of Italy, as well as Minister of Economy and Finance, since November 2011. Monti served as a European Commissioner from 1995 to 2004, with responsibility for the Internal Market, Services, Customs and Taxation from 1995 to 1999 and for Competition from 1999 to 2004. Monti has also been Rector and President of Bocconi University in Milan. On 12 November 2011, in the midst of the European sovereign debt crisis, Monti was invited by President Giorgio Napolitano to form a new technocratic government (in Italian, governo tecnico) following the resignation of Silvio Berlusconi. Monti was sworn in as Prime Minister on 16 November 2011, just a week after having been appointed a Senator for Life. [ Early life ] Monti was born in Varese on 19 March 1943. He also served as president of the SUERF (The European Money and Finance Forum) from 1982 to 1985. In his office as a European Commissioner from 1994 to 1999, he was responsible for Internal Market, Financial Services and Financial Integration, Customs, and Taxation. His work with the commission has earned him the nickname &quot; Super Mario &quot; from his colleagues and from the press. [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/programmes/letter_from_america/3921303.stm Examining the EU executive BBC News 23 July 2004 13 February 2012 ] As Competition Commissioner Monti led the investigation into a number of high profile and controversial mergers, including: Scania AB &amp; Volvo (1999), [ http://www.europeanvoice.com/article/imported/monti-puts-brakes-on-eu-merger-mania/40395.aspx Monti puts brakes on EU merger mania European Voice 23 March 2000 13 February 2012 ] WorldCom &amp; Sprint (2000), [ http://news.cnet.com/2100-1033-243110.html Sprint, WorldCom call off $120 billion merger CNet News 13 July 2000 13 February 2012 ] General Electric &amp; Honeywell (2001), Schneider Electric &amp; Legrand (2001) [ http://www.economist.com/node/814366 A shocking denouement The Economist 11 October 2001 13 February 2012 ] and Carnival Corporation &amp; P &amp; O Ferries (2002). [ http://news.cnet.com/The-full-Monti/2100-1016_3-5178159.html The full Monti CNet News 24 March 2004 13 February 2012 ] Monti was also responsible for levying the EU&apos;s largest ever fine at the time (€497 million) against Microsoft for abusing its dominant market position in 2004. [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/3563697.stm Microsoft hit by record EU fine BBC News 24 March 2004 13 February 2012 ] Monti was criticised in the media and by competition lawyers for the perceived inflexibility of the merger oversight process and the high number of cases that were being blocked. [ http://www.economist.com/node/387773 Spaghetti Monti The Economist 5 October 2000 13 February 2012 ] [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/business/2385037.stm Mario Monti: Merger man on a mission BBC News 1 November 2002 13 February 2012 ] On 1 November 2002, Monti responding to the European Court of Justice&apos;s ruling which reversed his decision to block the merger between Airtours &amp; First Choice Holidays said, &quot; Last week was a tough week for the Commission&apos;s merger control policy - and of course for me. &quot; This ruling in combination with his decision to block the General Electric &amp; Honeywell merger led to criticism in the United States against both the Commission&apos;s procedures and accusations that Monti&apos;s decisions were politically motivated. [ http://www.economist.com/node/905116 Monti braves the catcalls The Economist 13 December 2001 13 February 2012 ] Monti, however, was defended by supporters who saw his actions as an important step in the development of competition law in the EU. Dan Rubinfeld, economics professor at the University of California who worked on the US Justice Department&apos;s antitrust case against Microsoft, said of Monti, &quot; There has been a lot of talk of politics in this and other cases, but I believe he has been driven entirely by the desire to do the right thing. &quot; On 11 December 2002, Monti proposed a series of reforms to the EU&apos;s merger rules and made structural changes within the Commission&apos;s Competition department which aimed to improve transparency for companies throughout the merger review process. [ http://www.europeanvoice.com/article/imported/monti-unveils-merger-reform-proposals-based-on-us-model/46333.aspx Monti unveils merger reform proposals based on US model European Voice 12 December 2002 13 February 2012 ] The reforms were adopted by the EU as Regulation 139/2004 (known as ECMR). In 2004, Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi returned to power in Italy and did not re-appoint Monti to the Barroso Commission. The reforms are intended to open certain professions (such as taxi drivers, pharmacists, doctors, lawyers and notaries) to more competition by reforming their licensing systems and abolishing minimum tariffs for their services. [ http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/business-16653863 Italy approves sweeping reforms BBC News 20 January 2012 13 February 2012 ] [ http://www.ft.com/cms/s/0/b13df170-4392-11e1-adda-00144feab49a.html#axzz1mFlcNTKs Monti unveils liberalisation plans Financial Times 20 January 2012 13 February 2012 ] Article 18 of Italy&apos;s labour code, which requires companies that employ 15 or more workers to re-hire (rather than compensate) any employee found to have been fired without just cause, [ http://www.economist.com/node/1205271 Berlusconi 2, Cofferati 1 The Economist 27 June 2002 13 February 2012 ] [ http://www.reuters.com/article/2012/02/02/us-italy-reforms-idUSTRE8111TF20120202 Italy govt to pass labour reforms even without unions Reuters 2 February 2012 13 February 2012 ] would also be reformed. The reforms to Article 18 are intended to make it easier for companies to dismiss or lay-off employees, which would hopefully encourage companies to hire more employees on permanent rather than short-term renewable contracts. The proposals have been met by strong opposition from labour unions and public protests.
