In the English -speaking world, &quot; Provençal &quot; is often used to refer to all dialects of Occitan, but it actually refers specifically to the dialect spoken in Provence. &quot; Provençal &quot; (with &quot; Limousin &quot; ) is also the customary name given to the older version of the langue d&apos;oc used by the troubadour s of medieval literature, corresponding to Old French or the langue d&apos;oïl of the northern areas of France. In 2007, the ISO 639-3 code changed from prv to oci, as prv was merged into oci. [ Grammar ] Written in mistralian norm ( &quot; normo mistralenco &quot; ) the definite articles are masculine lou, feminine la, and plural li (lis before vowels). Nouns and adjectives, the Latin masculine endings have mostly dropped, but -e remains, while the feminine ending is -o. Nouns do not inflect for number, but all adjectives ending in vowels (-e or -o) become -i, and all plural adjectives take -s before vowels: lo bon ami &quot; the good friend &quot; (masc.), la bono amigo &quot; the good friend &quot; (fem.), li bons ami &quot; the good friends &quot; (masc.), li bonis amigo &quot; the good friends &quot; (fem.). Written in classical norm ( &quot; norma classica &quot; ) the definite articles are masculine lo, feminine la, and plural lis. Nouns and adjectives, the Latin masculine endings have mostly dropped, but -e remains, while the feminine ending is -a. Nouns do inflect for number, all adjectives ending in vowels (-e or -a) become -i, and all plural adjectives take -s: lo bon amic &quot; the good friend &quot; (masc.), la bona amiga &quot; the good friend &quot; (fem.), lis bons amics &quot; the good friends &quot; (masc.), lis bonis amigas &quot; the good friends &quot; (fem.). The prononciation always remains the same, the two norms (mistralian and classical) are only two different ways to write the same language. [ Literature ] Modern Provençal literature was given impetus by Nobel laureate Frédéric Mistral and the association Félibrige he founded with other writers, such as Théodore Aubanel. The beginning of the 20th Century saw other authors like Joseph d&apos;Arbaud and Valère Bernard. It has been enhanced and modernized since the second half of the 20th Century by writers such as Robèrt Lafont, Pierre Pessemesse, Claude Barsotti, Max-Philippe Delavouët, Philippe Gardy, Florian Vernet, Danielle Julien, Jòrgi Gròs, Sèrgi Bec, Bernat Giély, and many others.
