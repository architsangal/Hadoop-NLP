The population was recorded as 11,700 by the 2010 census. It is the county seat of Nelson County. [ 6 ] It is named for David Bard, the man who obtained the land for the city from the governor of Virginia, and his brother William Bard, the surveyor who laid out the town. [ History ] St. Joseph Proto-Cathedral in Bardstown Bardstown is one of the oldest cities in Kentucky settled by European Americans. { History of Bardstown steeped in bourbon } { Courier Journal April 4, 2007 } Settled in the 1780s, it was chartered in 1790. Reflecting the westward migration of Americans after the Revolutionary War, Bardstown was the first center of Catholicism west of the Appalachian Mountains in the original territory of the United States. { Jay P. Dolan, The American Catholic Experience: A History from Colonial Times to the Present (Notre Dame, Indiana: Notre Dame University Press, 1992), pp. 119, 160-61. } The Diocese of Bardstown was established on February 8, 1808, to serve all Catholics between the Appalachians and the Mississippi River, an area now served by 44 diocese s and archdiocese s in 10 states. Its cathedral still stands as the Basilica of Saint Joseph Proto-Cathedral. The seat of the diocese was transferred to Louisville in 1841. { Dolan, American Catholic Experience, p. 161. } Bardstown is still the home of a Catholic high school, Bethlehem High School. The Old Talbott Tavern, built in 1779 and located just off the Courthouse Square in the center of Bardstown, is part of Bardstown&apos;s rich history. Several notable Americans have passed through the tavern&apos;s doors, including Abraham Lincoln and Daniel Boone. { Discoverourtown.com - Old Talbott Tavern } Bullet holes in an upstairs wall are reputed to have been put there by Jesse James. { Talbotts.com - History } Some of the people who stayed at the tavern in years past are rumored never to have checked out, even after death, as people claim to have encountered ghosts or other paranormal activity at the tavern. Bardstown is the home of My Old Kentucky Home State Park. On their plantation, Judge John Rowan and his wife Ann Lytle Rowan built &quot; Federal Hill, &quot; the mansion that is mistakenly alleged to have inspired his cousin Stephen Foster to write the song &quot; My Old Kentucky Home. &quot; Federal Hill is depicted on the reverse of the Kentucky state quarter issued by the United States Mint in 2002. Several distilleries operate in and around Bardstown, including Jim Beam, Maker&apos;s Mark, and Heaven Hill. The regional production of bourbon has generated the annual Kentucky Bourbon Festival, whose promoters have trademark ed the phrase Bourbon Capital of the World ® to apply exclusively to Bardstown. { Kentucky Bourbon Festival web site. } The local tourism commission promotes the use of the trademarked phrase. { Bardstown Tourism Commission web site. } A public museum, the Oscar Getz Museum of Whiskey, showcases this aspect of local history. Bardstown&apos;s downtown area is a historic district listed on the National Register of Historic Places. Bardstown, along with Nelson County, is part of the Louisville Metropolitan Statistical Area. [ Transportation ] The Bluegrass Parkway is a limited-access highway that passes just south of Bardstown. A part of the Kentucky parkway system, the highway was formerly a toll road, but tolls were removed in 1991 when enough tolls were collected to pay off its construction bonds. Railroad freight service is provided by the R.J. Corman Railroad/Central Kentucky Lines, over the former Bardstown Branch of the Louisville and Nashville Railroad. Corman operates My Old Kentucky Dinner Train, a passenger train specializing in dinner service that travels the line from the historic Bardstown depot to Clermont and back. [ Attractions and events ] My Old Kentucky Home [ http://www.visitbardstown.com/tourism/civilWar.html Bardstown, Kentucky - Civil War Attractions Visitbardstown.com 2009-07-13 ] The Civil War Museum in Bardstown is the fourth-largest Civil War museum in the country. The Kentucky Bourbon Festival celebrates Bardstown&apos;s history in the production of bourbon. It was designated Kentucky &apos;s official bourbon festival by the Kentucky General Assembly in 2000. The primary jump plane was a 1955 DHC-2 Beaver, which held 8 jumpers at a time.
