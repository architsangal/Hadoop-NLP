It has the traditional name Rasalgethi or Ras Algethi ( [ رأس الجاثي ra&apos;s al-jaθiyy ] Head of the Kneeler), and the Flamsteed designation 64 Herculis. [ Constellations: Hercules &apos;the Strongman&apos; The BBC (British Broadcasting Corporation) Kurt Vonnegut http://www.bbc.co.uk/dna/h2g2/A29778916 2010-11-14 ] The traditional name &quot; Head &quot; comes from the fact that in antiquity Hercules was depicted upside down on the constellation maps. The term ra&apos;s al-jaθiyy or Ras al Djathi was appeared in the catalogue of stars in the Calendarium of Al Achsasi al Mouakket, which was translated into Latin as Caput Ingeniculi. [ Knobel E. B. Al Achsasi Al Mouakket, on a catalogue of stars in the Calendarium of Mohammad Al Achsasi Al Mouakket Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society 55 429 June 1895 1895MNRAS..55..429K ] In Chinese astronomy, Rasalgethi is called 帝座, Pinyin : Dìzuò, meaning Emperor&apos;s Seat, because this star is marking itself and stand alone in Emperor&apos;s Seat asterism, Heavenly Market enclosure (see : Chinese constellation ). { AEEA (Activities of Exhibition and Education in Astronomy) 天文教育資訊網 2006 年 6 月 25 日 } 帝座 (Dìzuò) was westernized into Ti Tso by R.H. Allen, with the same meaning { Richard Hinckley Allen: Star Names — Their Lore and Meaning: Hercules } [ Properties ] The angular diameter of the red giant, α 1 Her, has been measured with an interferometer as34  ±  0.8 mas, or 0.034  arcseconds. [ Benson, J. A.; Dyck, H. M.; Mason, W. L.; Howell, R. R.; Ridgway, S. T.; Dixon, D. J. The infrared angular diameter of Alpha Herculis measured with a Michelson interferometer Astronomical Journal 1991 December 102 pp.2091–2097 PDF 10.1086/116033 1991AJ....102.2091B ] At an estimated distance of 110 parsec s from Hipparcos, this corresponds to a radius of about 280 million kilometers (or 170 million miles), which is roughly 400 R ☉ or 1.87 AU. To determine the Rasalgheti&apos;s radius in terms of solar units, the calculations begin with the formula for angular diameter as follows: \begin{smallmatrix} {\delta} = \frac{d_R}{D_R}\end{smallmatrix} where δ equals Rasalgheti&apos;s angular diameter in arcseconds, d R the star&apos;s diameter in AU, and D R the Distance from Earth in parsecs. If we know the angular diameter and the Distance, then we can solve for d R as follows: \begin{smallmatrix}d_R = \delta \cdot D_R = {0.034} \cdot 110.0 = 3.740 AU \end{smallmatrix}. To obtain Rasalgheti&apos;s radius: \begin{smallmatrix}R_R = {\left ( {\frac {d_R}{2}} \right )} = {\left ( {\frac {3.740}{2}} \right )} = 1.870 AU \end{smallmatrix}. Converting into Solar units, 1 AU = 149,597,871  km and the mean radius of the Sun = 696,000  km, hence the calculation: \begin{smallmatrix}d_B = {\left ( 1.87 AU \right )} {\left ( {\frac {149,597,871 km}{696,000 km}} \right )} = 280,000,000 km = 402 R_{\odot} \end{smallmatrix} (rounded). If Rasalgheti were at the center of our Solar System its radius would extend past the orbit of Mars at 1.5 AU but not quite as far as the asteroid belt. The red giant is estimated to have started its life with about 7-8 solar mass es. Like most type M stars near the end of their lives, Rasalgethi is experiencing a high degree of stellar mass loss creating a sparse, gaseous envelope that extends at least 90 astronomical unit s. [ Deutsch Armin J. The Circumstellar Envelope of Alpha Herculis Astrophysical Journal 1956 March 123 pp.210–227 PDF 10.1086/146152 1956ApJ...123..210D ] [ Star system ] When viewed through a telescope, this system is resolved into two components designated α 1 and α 2. These two components are more than 500 astronomical units apart, with an estimated orbital period of approximately 3600 years. α 1 is a relatively massive red bright giant. α 2 is actually a double star system with a primary yellow giant star and a secondary, yellow-white dwarf star. (These components are sometimes designated α Herculis A, Ba and Bb, respectively.)
