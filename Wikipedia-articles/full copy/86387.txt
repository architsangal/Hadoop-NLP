(by) (4).jpg } { 200px } { Myotis myotis } { Animal ia } { Chordata } { Mammal ia } { Chiroptera } { Microchiroptera } { Vespertilionidae } { Gray, 1821 } { Subfamilies } { Vespertilioninae } { Murininae } { Myotinae } { Kerivoulinae } Vesper bats (family Vespertilionidae), also known as evening bats or common bats, are the largest and best-known family of bat s. They belong to the suborder Microchiroptera ( microbat s ). Over three hundred species are distributed all over the world, on every continent except Antarctica. It owes its name to the Latin word vespertilio ( &quot; bat &quot; ), from vesper, meaning &quot; evening &quot;. [ History ] Molecular data indicate Vespertilionidae diverged from Molossidae in the early Eocene period. [ Miller-Butterworth, C.M., et al. 2007 A Family Matter: Conclusive Resolution of the Taxonomic Position of the Long-Fingered Bats, Miniopterus Molecular Biology and Evolution 24 7 1553–1561 10.1093/molbev/msm076 17449895 ] The family is thought to have originated somewhere in Laurasia, possibly North America. [ Teeling, E.C., et al. 2005 A Molecular Phylogeny for Bats Illuminates Biogeography and the Fossil Record Science 307 no. 5709 pp. 580–584 10.1126/science.1105113 15681385 ] [ Characteristics ] Almost all vesper bats are insectivore s, exceptions being some Myotis and Pizonyx that catch fish and the larger Nyctalus species that have been known on occasion to catch small passerine birds in flight. The dental formula of vesper bats varies between species: [ 1-2.1.1-3.3 2-3.1.2-3.3 ] They rely mainly on echolocation, but they lack the enlarged noses that some microbats have to improve the ultrasound beam, and instead &quot; shout &quot; through their open mouths to project their ultrasound beams. In compensation, many species have relatively large ears. As a group, vesper bats cover the full gamut of flight ability, with the relatively weak flying Pipistrellus that have fluttery, almost insect -like flight to the long-winged and fast-flying genera such as Lasiurus and Nyctalus. The family size range is from [ 3 to 13 cm in on ] in length, excluding the tail, which is itself quite long in most species. They are generally brown or grey in color, but some have brightly colored fur, with reds, oranges, and yellows all being known, and many having white patches or stripes. [ Macdonald, D. 1984 The Encyclopedia of Mammals Facts on File New York 807 0-87196-871-1 ] Most species roost in caves, although some make use of hollow trees, rocky crevices, animal burrow s, or other forms of shelter. Colony sizes also vary greatly, with some roosting alone, and others in groups up to a million individuals. Species native to temperate latitudes typically hibernate, while a few of the tropical species aestivate.
