Most decapods are scavenger s. It is estimated that the order contains nearly 15,000 species in around 2,700 genera, with approximately 3,300 fossil species. The earliest fossil decapod is the Devonian Palaeopalaemon. Crean January 2, 2010 November 14, 2004 ] [ Anatomy ] [ Decapod anatomy ] As the name Decapoda (from the Greek [ el δέκα ], [ el deca- ], &quot; ten &quot;, and [ el πούς / ποδός ], -pod, &quot; foot &quot; ) implies, all decapods have ten legs. These are in the form of five pairs of thoracic appendages on the last five thoracic segments. The front three pairs function as mouthparts and are generally referred to as maxilliped s; the remainder are pereiopod s. In many decapods, however, one pair of legs has enlarged pincers; the claws are called chelae, so those legs may be called chelipeds. Further appendages are found on the abdomen, with each segment capable of carrying a pair of biramous pleopod s, the last of which form part of the tail fan (together with the telson ) and are called uropod s. [ Classification ] Classification within the order Decapoda depends on the structure of the gill s and legs, and the way in which the larva e develop, giving rise to two suborders: Dendrobranchiata and Pleocyemata. Dendrobranchiata consists of prawn s, including many species colloquially referred to as &quot; shrimp &quot;, such as the &quot; white shrimp &quot;, Litopenaeus setiferus. Pleocyemata includes the remaining groups, including true shrimp. Those groups which usually walk rather than swim (Pleocyemata, excluding Stenopodidea and Caridea) form a clade called Reptantia.
