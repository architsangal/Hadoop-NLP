For the museum in Caerleon see Roman Baths Museum The Roman Baths complex is a site of historical interest in the English city of Bath. The house is a well-preserved Roman site for public bathing. The Roman Baths themselves are below the modern street level. There are four main features: the Sacred Spring, the Roman Temple, the Roman Bath House and the Museum holding finds from Roman Bath. The buildings above street level date from the 19th century. The Baths are a major tourist attraction and, together with the Grand Pump Room, receive more than one million visitors a year, [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3DB774A562-65BF-7E43-3224A238E485A13C Conservation in Action at the Roman Bath 2007-11-01 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] with 1,037,518 people during 2009. [ Visits made in 2009 to visitor attractions http://www.alva.org.uk/visitor_statistics/ Association of leading visitor attractions ] It was featured on the 2005 TV program Seven Natural Wonders as one of the wonders of the West Country. Visitors can see the Baths and Museum but cannot enter the water. An audio guide is available in several languages. In 2009 a grant of £90,000 was made to Bath and North East Somerset Council to contribute towards the cost of re-developing displays and improving access to the Roman Baths, [ Morris Steven Bath cleansing brings Romans back to life http://www.guardian.co.uk/travel/2010/sep/09/bath-roman-baths-overhaul 10 September 2010 Guardian 2010-09-10 ] by the Department for Culture, Media and Sport /Wolfson Fund, which was established to promote improvements in Museums and Galleries in England. [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/media/news/2008/August/Pages/Major%20redevelopment%20grant%20awarded%20to%20the%20Roman%20Baths.aspx Major redevelopment grant awarded to the Roman Baths 2008-08-27 Press Release Bath and North East Somerset 2008-08-29 ] [ April 2012 H3llBot ] [ http://www.culture.gov.uk/images/publications/088-successful-institutions-and-projects-table.pdf Summary of the successful institutions and projects 2008-08-26 PDF Department for Culture, Media and Sport 2008-08-29 ] [ How the hot springs form at Bath ] The water which bubbles up from the ground at Bath fell as rain on the nearby Mendip Hills. It percolates down through limestone aquifer s to a depth of between [ 2700 m ft -2 ] and [ 4300 m ft -2 ] where geothermal energy raises the water temperature to between [ 64 °C °F 1 on ] and [ 96 °C °F 1 ]. Under pressure, the heated water rises to the surface along fissures and faults in the limestone. This process is similar to an artificial one known as Enhanced Geothermal System which also makes use of the high pressures and temperatures below the Earth&apos;s crust. Hot water at a temperature of [ 46 °C °F 1 ] rises here at the rate of [ 1170000 l impgal 0 ] every day, [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3DF9F320C4-1A95-4C04-AC609094E5B5DFD3 Sacred Spring 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] from a geological fault (the Pennyquick fault). In 1983 a new spa water bore-hole was sunk, providing a clean and safe supply of spa water for drinking in the Pump Room. [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3D4B6F21CE-7CF4-4283-AF5C03FB05527814 Hot Water 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] [ History ] { Bladud overlooking the King&apos;s Bath carries the date of 1699, but its inclusion in earlier pictures shows that it is much older than this. } [ Tindall Laurence Roman Baths King Bladud 1982 http://www.laurencetindall.co.uk/page98.html 2008-10-25 ] The first shrine at the site of the hot springs was built by Celts, [ http://www.somersettouristguide.com/Bath/The_Roman_Baths_722.asp The Roman Baths 2007-11-01 Somerset Tourist Guide ] and was dedicated to the goddess Sulis, whom the Roman s identified with Minerva. Geoffrey of Monmouth in his largely fictional Historia Regum Britanniae describes how in 836 BC the spring was discovered by the British king Bladud who built the first baths. [ Geoffrey of Monmouth Thorpe, Lewis G. M. (trans) The History of the Kings of Britain Penguin Harmondsworth, England 1966 80 0-14-044170-0 ] Early in the 18th century Geoffrey&apos;s obscure legend was given great prominence as a royal endorsement of the waters&apos; qualities, with the embellishment that the spring had cured Bladud and his herd of pigs of leprosy through wallowing in the warm mud. [ Borsay Peter The Image of Georgian Bath, 1700-2000 Oxford University Press Oxford, England 2000 49–55 0-19-820265-2 ] [ Roman use ] The name Sulis continued to be used after the Roman invasion, leading to the town&apos;s Roman name of Aquae Sulis (literally, &quot; the waters of Sulis &quot; ). The temple was constructed in 60-70 AD and the bathing complex was gradually built up over the next 300 years. [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/worldheritage/2.3Des.htm City of Bath World Heritage Site Management Plan 2007-11-01 Bath and North East Somerset http://web.archive.org/web/20070614100836/http%3A//www.bathnes.gov.uk/worldheritage/2.3Des.htm 2007-06-14 ] During the Roman occupation of Britain, and possibly on the instructions of Emperor Claudius, [ http://www.plumbingworld.com/historyroman.html The History of Plumbing - Roman and English Legacy 2007-11-01 Plumbing World ] engineers drove oak piles to provide a stable foundation into the mud and surrounded the spring with an irregular stone chamber lined with lead. In the 2nd century it was enclosed within a wooden barrel-vaulted building, and included the caldarium (hot bath), tepidarium (warm bath), and frigidarium (cold bath). [ http://www.timetravel-britain.com/05/April/romanbaths.shtml The Roman Baths 2007-11-01 TimeTravel Britain ] After the Roman withdrawal from Britain in the first decade of the 5th century, these fell into disrepair and were eventually lost due to silting up, [ http://www.birminghamuk.com/romanbaths.htm The Roman Baths 2007-11-01 BirminghamUk.com ] and flooding. [ Havinden Michael The Somerset Landscape Hodder and Stoughton London The making of the English landscape 67 0-340-20116-9 1981 ] The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle suggests the original Roman baths were destroyed in the 6th century. [ Bayley Stephen 2007 September Is Bath Britain&apos;s most backward city? The Observer http://arts.guardian.co.uk/art/architecture/story/0%2C%2C2170069%2C00.html 2007-11-01 ] About 130 curse tablet s have been found.Many of the curses related to thefts of clothes whilst the victim was bathing. { Roger Tomlin (1988), Tabellae Sulis: Roman inscribed tablets of tin and lead from the sacred spring at Bath, Oxford.. } This collection is the most important found in Britain. [ Redevelopment ] The Great Bath — the entire structure above the level of the pillar bases is a later reconstruction. The baths have been modified on several occasions, including the 12th century when John of Tours built a curative bath over the King&apos;s Spring reservoir and the 16th century when the city corporation built a new bath (Queen&apos;s Bath) to the south of the Spring. [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/worldheritage/3Append.htm City of Bath World Heritage Site Management Plan - Appendix 3 2007-11-01 Bath and North East Somerset Council http://web.archive.org/web/20070804014112/http%3A//www.bathnes.gov.uk/worldheritage/3Append.htm 2007-08-04 ] The spring is now housed in 18th century buildings, designed by architects John Wood, the Elder and John Wood, the Younger, father and son. Visitors drank the waters in the Grand Pump Room, a neo-classical salon which remains in use, both for taking the waters and for social functions. Victorian expansion of the baths complex followed the neo-classical tradition established by the Woods. In 1810 the Hot Springs failed and William Smith opened up the Hot Bath Spring to the bottom, where he found that the spring had not failed but had flowed into a new channel. Smith restored the water to its original course and the Baths filled in less time than formerly. [ http://www.brlsi.org/wsmith.htm William Smith - A brief survey of his work in the Bath 2007-11-01 Bath Royal Literary and Scientific Instituition http://web.archive.org/web/20071013223811/http%3A//www.brlsi.org/wsmith.htm 2007-10-13 ] The visitor entrance is via an 1897 concert hall by J M Brydon. It is an eastward continuation of the Grand Pump Room with a glass-domed centre and single-storey radiused corner. [ Concert Hall Images of England http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id%3D442115 2007-10-30 ] The Grand Pump Room was begun in 1789 by Thomas Baldwin. He resigned in 1791 and John Palmer continued the scheme until its completion in 1799. The elevation on to Abbey Church Yard has a centre piece of four engaged Corinthian columns with entablatures and pediment. It has been designated by English Heritage as a grade I listed building. [ Grand Pump Room Images of England http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id%3D442115 2007-10-30 ] The north colonnade was also designed by Thomas Baldwin. [ North Colonnade at Grand Pump Room Images of England http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id%3D442111 2007-10-30 ] The south colonnade is similar but had an upper floor added in the late 19th century. [ South Colonnade at Grand Pump Room Images of England http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id%3D442112 2007-10-30 ] The museum and Queen&apos;s Bath including the &quot; Bridge &quot; spanning York Street to the City Laundry were by Charles Edward Davis in 1889. It comprises a southward extension to the Grand Pump Room, in which some remains of the C17 Queen&apos;s Bath are merged. [ Museum &amp; Queen&apos;s Bath including &quot; Bridge &quot; spanning York Street to City Laundry Images of England http://www.imagesofengland.org.uk/details/default.aspx?id%3D442116 2007-10-30 ] [ Museum ] The museum houses artefacts from the Roman period including objects which were thrown into the Sacred Spring, presumably as offerings to the goddess. These include more than 12,000 Roman currency coins which is the largest collective votive deposit known from Britain. [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3DC0E83055-8546-40AF-929D292F81B86E20 Objects from the spring 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] A gilt bronze head of the goddess Sulis Minerva, which was discovered nearby in 1727, is displayed. [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3D80FE53CD-5D86-475E-89B9A476314E8892 Minerva&apos;s Head 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] Gorgon&apos;s Head from the Temple Pediment The Bath Roman Temple stood on a podium more than two metres above the surrounding courtyard, approached by a flight of steps. On the approach there were four large, fluted Corinthian column s supporting a frieze and decorated pediment above. The pediment, parts of which are displayed in the museum, is the triangular ornamental section, [ 26 ft m 1 ] wide and [ 8 ft m 1 ] from the apex to the bottom, [ Richmond I. A. J. M. C. Toynbee 1955 The Temple of Sulis-Minerva at Bath The Journal of Roman Studies 45 97 10.2307/298749 The Journal of Roman Studies, Vol. 45 298749 97–105 ] above the pillars on the front of the building. It featured the very powerful central image of the Gorgon ’s head glowering down from a height of 15 metres on all who approached the temple. In early 2010 various stones on the pediment were conserved and rearranged. [ http://www.minervaconservation.com/projects/bath.htm Bath Roman Baths repairs by Minerva Minerva Stone Conservation January 19, 2010 ] In the corners of the pediment are Tritons, half men and half fish, servants of the water god Neptune. In the lower left centre ground is a face helmet in the form of a dolphin’s head. The small owl tucked away to the lower right of the large central roundel is also almost certainly perched atop another helmet. The central head is held aloft by female &apos;Victories&apos;, on a shield ringed with oak leaves. The Victories stand on globes. Above all this, in the apex of the pediment, is a star. The great head itself has snakes entwined within its beard, wings above its ears, beetling brows and a heavy moustache [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/media/press%2Breleases/2006/lifeandleisure/New%2Baddition%2Bto%2BGorgons%2Bhead.htm New addition to Gorgon’s head 2007-11-01 Bath and North East Somerset Council http://web.archive.org/web/20071204082519/http%3A//www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/media/press%2Breleases/2006/lifeandleisure/New%2Baddition%2Bto%2BGorgons%2Bhead.htm December 4, 2007 ] although there is some controversy about what this really represents as Gorgon s are usually female. [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3DBC2C8F00-61EA-4D00-841E578908654FC5 The Gorgon&apos;s head 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] An alternative interpretation sees the central head as the image of a water god such as the image of Oceanus, and yet another as a Celtic sun god. Also on display are the remains of the elaborate hypocaust heating system which served the sweat rooms. [ Conservation ] Statues on the terrace The late 19th century carvings of Roman Emperors and Governors of Roman Britain on the terrace overlooking the Great Bath are particularly susceptible to the effect of acid rain and are being protected with a wash of a sacrificial shelter coat every few years. [ http://www.romanbaths.co.uk/index.cfm?fuseAction%3DSM.nav%26amp%3BUUID%3D3F37CDAD-E666-49BD-9AA8FC1D739985F8 Conserving the monument 2007-10-31 Roman Baths Museum Web Site ] Exhibits within the temple precincts are susceptible to warm air which had the effect of drawing corrosive salts out of the Roman stonework. To help reduce this, a new ventilation system was installed in 2006. [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/media/press%2Breleases/2006/lifeandleisure/Temple%2Bprecinct%2Bwork%2Bcomplete.htm Temple precinct work complete 2007-11-01 Bath and North East Somerset Council http://web.archive.org/web/20071203202022/http%3A//www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/media/press%2Breleases/2006/lifeandleisure/Temple%2Bprecinct%2Bwork%2Bcomplete.htm December 3, 2007 ] [ Water safety ] Bath was charged with responsibility for the hot springs in a Royal Charter of 1591 granted by Elizabeth I. This duty has now passed to Bath and North East Somerset Council, who carry out monitoring of pressure, temperature and flow rates. The thermal waters contain sodium, calcium, chloride and sulphate ions in high concentrations. [ http://www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/environmentandplanning/hotsprings/ Bath Hot Springs - Protection and Water Monitoring 2007-11-01 Bath and North East Somerset Council http://web.archive.org/web/20071027140014/http%3A//www.bathnes.gov.uk/BathNES/environmentandplanning/hotsprings/ October 27, 2007 ] The water that flows through the Roman Baths is considered unsafe for bathing, partly due to its having passed through the still-functioning original lead pipes, and up until World War II, it was advertised on the basis of the radioactivity it contained. However the more significant danger is now considered to be infectious diseases. In 1979 a girl swimming in the restored bath swallowed some of the source water, and died five days later from amoebic meningitis. [ http://www.bbc.co.uk/dna/h2g2/A3023074 The City of Bath, Somerset, UK 2007-11-01 H2G2 ] Tests showed that a species of amoeba, Naegleria fowleri, [ Kilvington Simon John Beeching 1995 June Identification and epidemiological typing of Naegleria fowleri with DNA probes Applied and Environmental Microbiology 61 6 2071–2078 http://aem.asm.org/cgi/reprint/61/6/2071.pdf 2007-10-31 7793928 167479 ] was in the water and the pool was closed, and remains closed today. The newly-constructed Thermae Bath Spa nearby, designed by Nicholas Grimshaw and Partners, and the refurbished Cross Bath allow modern-day bathers to experience the waters via a series of more recently-drilled boreholes.
