Hadland (2005) } List of Norwegian monarchs begins traditionally in 872, when Harald I of Norway, known as Harald Fairhair, merged several, but not all of the petty kingdoms of Norway together with that of his father, Agder. This entity became, with later losses and expansions, the Kingdom of Norway. Harald founded thus the first royal house on Norway&apos;s throne, the Fairhair dynasty. However, the house became patrilineally extinct already with Harald&apos;s grandson Harald II Eiriksson († 970). { Aschehoug s Norgeshistorie, volum 2, pagina 92. } Norway&apos;s throne was in 1450 inherited by Christian I and thereafter possessed by Danish monarchs until 1814, bringing the kingdoms of Norway and of Denmark into a personal union. [ http://www.kongehuset.no/c27269/seksjonstekst/vis.html?tid%3D27680 History The Royal Court 2008-06-17 ] In 1814, Norway gained constitutional independence with a constitutional monarchy, ending the absolutism of 1660, and elected the Danish king&apos;s son, the later Christian VIII of Denmark, as Norway&apos;s King. After several months, however, the King was forced to abdicate, and the throne was given to Charles XIII of Sweden (Charles II in Norway), bringing the kingdoms of Norway and of Sweden into a personal union. [ http://www.kongehuset.no/c27320/seksjonstekst/vis.html?tid%3D28691 Union with Sweden in 1814 The Royal Court 2008-06-17 ] In 1905, the union ended, and Norway has since then been ruled by members of the House of Schleswig-Holstein-Sonderburg-Glücksburg. Early regnal dates are traditional and of uncertain accuracy. Before the Norwegian Law of Succession was introduced in 1163, inheritance of the throne was an affair filled with deep conflicts, subsequently leading to the civil war era. The Kings of Norway since 1450 usually had the styles and titles ‘His Royal Majesty NN, by God&apos;s Grace King to Denmark, King to Norway, King of the Goths, King of the Wends, Duke to Sleswick, Duke to Holsatia, Duke to Ditmarsh, Duke to Stormarn, Count to Oldenburg, and Count to Delmenhorst ’. With the introduction of the constitutional monarchy in 1814, the style was modified to ‘by God&apos;s Grace and after the Kingdom&apos;s Constitution’, but was only briefly in use. The last king to use the style ‘by God&apos;s Grace’ was Haakon VII, who died in 1957. The King&apos;s title is formally Norway&apos;s King (Norges Konge) and not King of Norway (Konge av Norge).
