In his movies, he is known for his acrobatic fighting style, comic timing, use of improvised weapons, and innovative stunt s. Jackie Chan has been acting since the 1960s and has appeared in over 100 films. Chan has received stars on the Hong Kong Avenue of Stars and the Hollywood Walk of Fame. As a cultural icon, Chan has been referenced in various pop songs, cartoon s, and video games. An opera tically trained vocalist, Chan is also a Cantopop and Mandopop star, having released a number of albums and sung many of the theme songs for the films in which he has starred. In addition to acting, Chan pursued a career in the Hong Kong music industry, releasing a number of original albums beginning in 1984. He founded the Jackie Chan Charitable Organization in 1998, which, among other projects, offers scholarships to Hong Kong youths, and he worked as a goodwill ambassador for UNICEF. [ Early life ] Chan was born on 7 April 1954, in Hong Kong, China as Chan Kong-sang, to Charles and Lee-Lee Chan, refugees from the Chinese Civil War. He was nicknamed Paopao ( [ 炮炮 ], literally meaning &quot; Cannonball &quot; ) because the high-energy child was always rolling around. [ Biography of Jackie Chan Biography Hong Kong Film.net http://www.hkfilm.net/chanbio.htm 28 February 2012 ] Since his parents worked for the French Consul in Hong Kong, Chan spent his formative years within the grounds of the consul&apos;s residence in the Victoria Peak district. [ Biography of Jackie Chan Biography Tiscali http://www.talktalk.co.uk/entertainment/film/biography/artist/jackie-chan/biography/45 28 February 2012 ] Chan attended the Nah-Hwa Primary School on Hong Kong Island, where he failed his first year, after which his parents withdrew him from the school. In 1960, his father immigrated to Canberra, Australia, to work as the head cook for the American embassy, and Chan was sent to the China Drama Academy, a Peking Opera School run by Master Yu Jim-yuen. [ Jackie Chan Battles Illegal Wildlife Trade Celebrity Values http://www.celebrityvalues.com/jackie_chan.html 28 February 2012 ] Chan trained rigorously for the next decade, excelling in martial arts and acrobatics. [ Biography of Jackie Chan StarPulse http://www.starpulse.com/Actors/Chan%2C_Jackie/Biography/ 28 February 2012 ] He eventually became part of the Seven Little Fortunes, a performance group made up of the school&apos;s best students, gaining the stage name Yuen Lo in homage to his master. Chan became close friends with fellow group members Sammo Hung and Yuen Biao, the three of them later to be known as the Three Brothers or Three Dragons. [ Seven Little Fortunes Feature article LoveAsianFilm http://www.loveasianfilm.com/features/sevenlittlefortunes.html 28 February 2012 ] At the age of 8, he appeared with some of his fellow &quot; Little Fortunes &quot;, in the film Big and Little Wong Tin Bar (1962), with Li Li Hua playing his mother. Chan appeared with Li again the following year, in The Love Eterne (1963) and had a small role in King Hu &apos;s 1966 film, Come Drink with Me. [ Come Drink With Me (1966) Database entry Hong Kong Cinemagic http://www.hkcinemagic.com/en/movie.asp?id%3D2418%26amp%3Bshowmovfullcast%3D1 29 February 2012 ] In 1971, after an appearance as an extra in another Kong Fu film, A Touch of Zen, Chan began his adult career in the film industry, initially signing to Chu Mu&apos;s Great Earth Film Company. [ Film career ] [ Early exploits: 1976–1979 ] { stuntman in the } { Bruce Lee films &apos;&apos; } { Fist of Fury &apos;&apos; (1972) and &apos;&apos; } { Enter the Dragon &apos;&apos; (1973, pictured). } In 1976, Jackie Chan received a telegram from Willie Chan, a film producer in the Hong Kong film industry who had been impressed with Jackie&apos;s stuntwork. Willie Chan offered him an acting role in a film directed by Lo Wei. Lo had seen Chan&apos;s performance in the John Woo film Hand of Death (1976) and planned to model him after Bruce Lee with the film New Fist of Fury. His stage name was changed to Sing Lung ( [ 成龍 ], also transcribed as Cheng Long, [ http://kungfu.chinaa2z.com/kungfu/html/Kung%20Fu%20Star/2008/20081225/20081225165205590921/20081225172231339876.html Jackie Chan: Chinese Kung Fu Superstar ChinaA2Z.com lily http://web.archive.org/web/20090408082259/http%3A//kungfu.chinaa2z.com/kungfu/html/Kung%20Fu%20Star/2008/20081225/20081225165205590921/20081225172231339876.html 8 April 2009 29 February 2012 ] literally &quot; become the dragon &quot; ) to emphasise his similarity to Bruce Lee, whose stage name was Lei Siu-lung ( [ 李小龍 ], meaning &quot; Little Dragon &quot; ). The film was unsuccessful because Chan was not accustomed to Lee&apos;s martial arts style. Despite the film&apos;s failure, Lo Wei continued producing films with similar themes, resulting in little improvement at the box office. [ Jackie Chan, a martial arts success story Biography Fighting Master http://www.fightingmaster.com/actors/jackie/index.htm 29 February 2012 ] Chan&apos;s first major breakthrough was the 1978 film Snake in the Eagle&apos;s Shadow, shot while he was loaned to Seasonal Film Corporation under a two-picture deal. [ Jackie Chan Biography (an Asian perspective) Biography Ng Kwong Loong (JackieChanMovie.com) http://www.jackiechanmovie.com/profile/biography/bio.htm http://web.archive.org/web/20040402123601/http%3A//www.jackiechanmovie.com/profile/biography/bio.htm 2 April 2004 29 February 2012 ] Under director Yuen Woo-ping, Chan was allowed complete freedom over his stunt work. [ Pollard Mark Snake in the Eagle&apos;s Shadow Movie review Kung Fu Cinema http://www.kungfucinema.com/reviews/snake-in-the-eagles-shadow-1978 29 February 2012 ] Chan then starred in Drunken Master, which finally propelled him to mainstream success. [ Pollard Mark Drunken Master Movie review Kung Fu Cinema http://www.kungfucinema.com/reviews/drunken-master-1978 29 February 2012 ] Upon Chan&apos;s return to Lo Wei&apos;s studio, Lo tried to replicate the comedic approach of Drunken Master, producing Half a Loaf of Kung Fu and Spiritual Kung Fu. He also gave Chan the opportunity to co-direct The Fearless Hyena with Kenneth Tsang. When Willie Chan left the company, he advised Jackie to decide for himself whether or not to stay with Lo Wei. During the shooting of Fearless Hyena Part II, Chan broke his contract and joined Golden Harvest, prompting Lo to blackmail Chan with triads, blaming Willie for his star&apos;s departure. The dispute was resolved with the help of fellow actor and director Jimmy Wang Yu, allowing Chan to stay with Golden Harvest. [ Success of the action comedy genre: 1980–1987 ] { Police Story,&apos;&apos; nicknamed &quot; Glass Story &quot; for its stunt work, is set in a modern period. } Willie Chan had become Jackie&apos;s personal manager and firm friend, and has remained so for over 30 years. He was instrumental in launching Chan&apos;s international career, beginning with his first forays into the American film industry in the 1980s. His first Hollywood film was Battle Creek Brawl in 1980. Chan then played a minor role in the 1981 film The Cannonball Run, which grossed US$100  million worldwide. Despite being largely ignored by audiences in favour of established American actors like Burt Reynolds, Chan was impressed by the outtakes shown at the closing credits, inspiring him to include the same device in his future films. After the commercial failure of The Protector in 1985, Chan temporarily abandoned his attempts to break into the US market, returning his focus to Hong Kong films. Back in Hong Kong, Chan&apos;s films began to reach a larger audience in East Asia, with early successes in the lucrative Japanese market including The Young Master (1980) and Dragon Lord (1982). The Young Master went on to beat previous box office records set by Bruce Lee and established Chan as Hong Kong cinema&apos;s top star. With Dragon Lord, he began experimenting with elaborate stunt action sequences, [ Dragon Lord Love HK Film http://www.lovehkfilm.com/reviews_2/dragon_lord.htm 29 February 2012 ] including a pyramid fight scene that holds the record for the most take s for a single scene with 2900 takes, [ Dragon Lord (DVD Description) Amazon.com http://www.amazon.co.uk/dp/B0000A5BRV 29 February 2012 ] and the final fight scene where he performs various stunts, including one where he does a back flip off a loft and falls to the lower ground. [ Filmography and screen persona ] [ Jackie Chan filmography ] { 2008 Cannes Film Festival. } Jackie Chan created his screen persona as a response to Bruce Lee, and the numerous imitators who appeared before and after Lee&apos;s death. In contrast to Lee&apos;s characters, who were typically stern, morally upright heroes, Chan plays well-meaning, slightly foolish regular guys (often at the mercy of their friends, girlfriends or families) who always triumph in the end despite the odds. [ The Jackie Chan Charitable Foundation ] Founded in 1988, the Jackie Chan Charitable Foundation offers scholarships and active help to Hong Kong&apos;s young people through a variety of worthy causes. Since 2005, the Dragon&apos;s Heart Foundation has built over a dozen schools, provided books, fees, and uniforms, and has raised millions of dollars to give much-needed educational opportunities for the poor. In addition, the Dragon&apos;s Heart Foundation provides for the elderly with donations of warm clothing, wheelchairs, and other items. Jackie often travels to the remote locations to attend groundbreakings or school openings, and to lend support and encouragement.
