[ About DPR http://www.dprnigeria.com/aboutus.html ] { Governor of } { North-Eastern State of } { Nigeria } { August 1975 } { March 1976 } { Musa Usman } { None as State Became Defunct } [ 1942 12 17 ] { Katsina state, } { Nigeria } { Military/ } { Congress for Progressive Change (CPC) } { Islam } { Nigerian Army } { 1962 - 1985 } { Major General } Muhammadu Buhari (born December 17, 1942) was a Major General in the Nigerian Army and a former military ruler of Nigeria from December 31, 1983 to August 27, 1985. He also ran unsuccessfully for the office of the President of Nigeria in the 2003, 2007 and 2011 elections. His ethnic background is Fulani, and his faith is Islam; he is a native of Daura in Katsina State of Nigeria. [ Minister of Petroleum ] Having joined the army in 1962, Buhari first came to widespread public attention in 1976 when he became the Minister (or &quot; Federal Commissioner &quot; ) for Petroleum and Natural Resources under then- Head of State General Olusegun Obasanjo. Before then he served as Governor of the newly created North-Eastern State during the regime of Murtala Mohammed. He later became head of the newly created Nigerian National Petroleum Corporation { Troubled journey By Levi Akalazu Nwachuku, G. N. Uzoigwe } in 1977. [ April 2011 ] [ Buhari government ] Major-General Buhari was selected to lead the country by middle and high-ranking military officers after a successful military coup d&apos;etat that overthrew civilian President Shehu Shagari on December 31, 1983. At the time, Buhari was head of the Third Armored Division of Jos. { Matthews, Martin P. Nigeria: current issues and historical background. p. 121. } Buhari was appointed Head of State and Commander-in-Chief of the Armed Forces, and Tunde Idiagbon was appointed Chief of General Staff (the de facto No. 2 in the administration). Buhari justified the military&apos;s seizure of power by castigating the civilian government as hopelessly corrupt, and his administration subsequently initiated a public campaign against indiscipline known as &quot; War Against Indiscipline &quot; (WAI). Aspects of this campaign included public humiliation of civil servants who arrived late for work whilst guards were armed with whips to ensure orderly queues at bus stops. [ http://www.bbc.co.uk/news/world-africa-12890807 Nigeria&apos;s Muhammadu Buhari in profile BBC News 17 April 2011 2011-04-20 ] He also moved to silence critics of his administration, passing decrees curbing press freedoms and allowing for opponents to be detained up to three months without formal charges. { Falola, Toyin and Matthew M. Heaton. A History of Nigeria. p. 214. } He also banned strikes and lockouts by workers and founded Nigeria&apos;s first secret police force, the National Security Organization. { Matthews, Martin P. Nigeria: current issues and historical background. p. 122. } His government sentenced popular musician and political critic Fela Kuti to ten years in prison on charges that Amnesty International denounced as fabricated and politically motivated; [ Shola Adenekan http://www.guardian.co.uk/news/2006/feb/15/guardianobituaries.mainsection Obituary: Dr Beko Ransome-Kuti The Guardian 2006-02-15 2011-04-20 ] Kuti was later pardoned and released by Buhari&apos;s successor. { Falola, Toyin and Matthew M. Heaton. A History of Nigeria. p. 217. } In another high-profile incident that sparked a diplomatic incident with Britain, British officials found Buhari&apos;s former transportation minister drugged in a crate marked for shipment to Lagos. [ http://www.washingtonpost.com/world/former-military-ruler-muhammadu-buhari-gains-support-as-anti-corruption-candidate-in-nigeria/2011/04/06/AFqQ4UpC_story.html Former military ruler Muhammadu Buhari gains support as anti-corruption candidate in Nigeria The Washington Post 2011-04-06 2011-04-20 ] According to the BBC, &quot; Buhari&apos;s attempts to re-balance public finances by curbing imports led to many job losses and the closure of businesses. &quot; [ April 2011 ] These losses were accompanied by a rise in prices and a decline in living standards. Some may hold contrary view to this assertion and call it mischievous though, [ The Sins of Buhari...by Prof Tam David West (Abridged) Adetayo Bamiduro http://www.mindshiftmovement.com/2011/02/sins-of-buhariby-prof-tam-david-west.html 25 February 2011 ] because Buhari is admired by many for his uprightness and stand against corruption. His government is revered for its ability to keep the country afloat by making progress through sheer economic ingenuity even when it rejected IMF loan and refused to adopt IMF conditionalities to devalue the Naira. [ BUHARISM: Economic Theory and Political Economy Sanusi Lamido Sanusi http://www.nigerdeltacongress.com/barticles/buharism.htm July 2002 ] His government is praised for its gain in reducing inflation by refusing to devalue the nation&apos;s currency, the Nigerian Naira, curbing imports of needless goods, curtailing oil theft and using counter trade policy to barter seized illegally bunkered crude oil for needful goods like machineries, enabling it to export above its OPEC quota. [ http://www.britannica.com/EBchecked/topic/83801/Muhammad-Buhari Muhammad Buhari (head of state of Nigeria) - Britannica Online Encyclopedia Britannica.com 2011-04-20 ] Babangida brought many of Buhari&apos;s most vocal critics into his administration, including Fela Kuti&apos;s brother Beko Ransome-Kuti, a doctor who had led a strike against Buhari to protest declining health care services. Buhari was then detained in Benin City until 1988. Buhari&apos;s admirers believe that he was overthrown by corrupt elements in his government who were afraid of being brought to justice as his policies were beginning to yield tangible dividends in terms of public discipline, curbing corruption, lowering inflation, enhancing workforce and improving productivity. [ BUHARI AND IDIAGBON: A MISSED OPPORTUNITY FOR NIGERIA Max Siollun http://www.nigerdeltacongress.com/barticles/buhari_and_idiagbon_a_missed_opp.htm October 2003 ] [ Later years ] Buhari served as the Chairman of the Petroleum Trust Fund (PTF), a body created by the government of General Abacha, and funded from the revenue generated by the increase in price of petroleum products, to pursue developmental projects around the country. A 1998 report in New African praised the PTF under Buhari for its transparency, calling it a rare &quot; success story &quot;. [ Development: PTF - shining in the gloom http://www.africasia.com/archive/na/98_06/abcr0604.htm June 1998 ] However, the same report also noted that critics had questioned the PTF&apos;s allocation of 20% of its resources to the military, which the critics feared would not be accountable for the revenue. In 2003, Buhari contested the presidential election [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/africa/6508055.stm BBC News Nigeria: Facts and figures April 17, 2007 ] as the candidate of the All Nigeria People&apos;s Party (ANPP). He was defeated by the People&apos;s Democratic Party nominee, President Olusẹgun Ọbasanjọ, by a margin of more than eleven million votes. It was claimed by Buhari&apos;s supporters and other members of the opposition that in some states, like Ebonyi, there were more votes than there were registered voters. [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/africa/6192919.stm BBC News Profile: Muhammadu Buhari December 19, 2006 ] [ http://news.bbc.co.uk/1/hi/world/africa/2968567.stm BBC News High drama in Abuja April 23, 2003 ] Although some allegations of fraud were proven in the courts and the conduct of the election was criticized by the Commonwealth Observer Group, { http://www.thecommonwealth.org/shared_asp_files/GFSR.asp?NodeID=164967 } the consensus among Nigerians was that he should not waste his time in court as he did not have the necessary resources to &quot; buy &quot; himself justice [ November 2011 ]. Eventually, the same court also decided that the level of proven electoral fraud was not sufficient to affect the outcome of the election and to warrant the cancellation of the whole Presidential election. [ http://allafrica.com/stories/201003180375.html Buhari Joins Congress for Progressive Change Vanguard Emeka Mamah 18 March 2010 2011-04-22 ] Buhari was the CPC Presidential candidate in the 16 April 2011 general election, running against incumbent President Goodluck Jonathan of the People&apos;s Democratic Party (PDP), Mallam Nuhu Ribadu of Action Congress of Nigeria (ACN), and Ibrahim Shekarau of ANPP. They were the major contenders among 20 contestants. [ Summary of the 2011 Presidential election results http://www.inecnigeria.org/results/presidential/ ] He was running on an anti-corruption platform and pledged to remove immunity protections from government officials. He also gave support to enforcement of Sharia law in Nigeria&apos;s northern states, which had previously caused him political difficulties among Christian voters in the country&apos;s south. However, he remains a &quot; folk hero &quot; to some for his vocal opposition to corruption. Buhari won 12,214,853 votes, coming second to the incumbent president Goodluck Jonathan of the PDP, who polled 22,495,187 votes and was declared the winner.
