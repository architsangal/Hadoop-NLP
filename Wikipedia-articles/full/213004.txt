[ http://chestofbooks.com/food/household/Woman-Encyclopaedia-2/Miss-Laurence-Alma-Tadema.html Miss Laurence Alma-Tadema Unattributed 1910–1912 Every Woman&apos;s Encyclopaedia London S.N. 16 August 2011 ] She never married and died in a nursing home in London in 1940. [ Literary work ] Her first novel, Love&apos;s Martyr, was published in 1886. In addition to her own collections of stories and poems, which she often published herself, Alma-Tadema wrote two novels, songs and works on drama; she also made translations. The Orlando Project says about Alma-Tadema&apos;s writing that the &quot; characteristic tone is one of intense emotion, but in prose and verse she has the gift of compression &quot;. She contributed widely to periodicals, notably The Yellow Book, and also edited one herself. Some of Alma-Tadema&apos;s plays were successfully produced in Germany. [ Political activities ] Alma Tadema had a close association with Poland. She was secretary of the &quot; Poland and the Polish Victims Relief Fund &quot; from 1915 to 1939. She was an admirer and long-term associate of Ignacy Jan Paderewski both as far as his music and political activities were concerned, notably on Polish independence. [ http://www.usc.edu/dept/polish_music/PMJ/issue/4.2.01/paderewskichopin.html Chopin: A Discourse Ignacy Jan Paderewski. Translated by Laurence Alma Tadema. Originally published 1911. Winter 2001 Polish Music Journal 1521–6039 4 2 16 August 2011 ] Alma-Tadema maintained a long-correspondence from him from 1915 to the end of her life. Some of her papers are deposited with the Bodleian Library, University of Oxford. [ http://www.bodley.ox.ac.uk/dept/scwmss/wmss/online/modern/alma-tadema/alma-tadema.html Collection Level Description: Papers of Miss Laurence Alma-Tadema 16 August 2011 ] [ American Tour ] Alma-Tadema, who had socialist leanings, travelled to America in 1907–08 to tour the country widely. She gave a series of readings on the &quot; Meaning of Happiness, &quot; which proved exceedingly popular. She also spoke on the plight of the divided Poland and asked her audience to express their feelings for this cause.
